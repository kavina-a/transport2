The problem in public bus transport

  • Unpredictability — passengers cannot reliably know when the next bus will arrive
  • No single view — operators and regulators lack a unified picture of fleet position and performance
  • Inefficient resource use — bunching, gaps, and ad hoc scheduling reduce capacity and increase cost
  • Limited data — little structured data for route planning, subsidies, or ancillary business models
Keep tone factual. These are widely recognised pain points that justify an efficiency-first, data-first approach before introducing payments or booking.

Proposed solution overview

  • Interconnected centralised transport system — one operational view for regulators and participating operators
  • Smart bus stands — real-time arrival displays so passengers see when the next bus is due
  • Transponder-based live bus tracking — accurate, low-cost position and movement data
  • Route and schedule optimisation — data-driven timetables and dispatching
  • Data-driven operational insights — reporting for planning, policy, and future ancillary revenue
This is the high-level architecture. No booking or payment in phase one — only visibility, reliability, and data.

How the system works

  • Buses fitted with transponders report position and identity to a central platform
  • Central system processes data and computes estimated times of arrival at stops
  • Smart bus stands receive and display live arrival information to passengers
  • Operators and regulators use the same platform for monitoring, reporting, and optimisation
  • Data is stored for analytics, route design, and future ancillary services
Buses (transponders) → Central platform → ETAs → Smart stands & dashboards
Walk through the flow simply. Stress that the same data serves passengers (displays), operators (control), and government (planning and policy).

Why efficiency-first — no payments initially

  • Build trust first — citizens experience reliable, visible service before any change to fare or payment systems
  • Lower risk — no dependency on payment infrastructure, card issuance, or cash handling in phase one
  • Clear value — “when does my bus come?” is answered; operational and policy benefits are immediate
  • Foundation for later — robust data and operations make future ticketing, booking, or ancillary models easier and more credible
Address the deliberate choice not to include booking or payment in phase one. Frame it as prudent and policy-friendly.

Public value & business opportunities

  • Public value: Predictable journeys, less waiting, better information; data for evidence-based policy and subsidy design
  • Operator value: Better fleet utilisation, fewer complaints, data for schedule and route improvements
  • Ancillary opportunities: Advertising at smart stands; premium or branded services; data products (anonymised) for urban planning; future integration with ticketing and booking when appropriate
Tie back to the Sub-Committee’s focus on ancillary business models. Show that efficiency and data come first, then revenue options follow.

Pilot rollout vision

  • Phase one (pilot): One or two high-demand corridors; transponders on participating buses; smart stands at key stops; central dashboard for operators and regulators
  • Metrics: Punctuality, headway regularity, passenger feedback, and data quality
  • Scale: Expand to more routes and operators based on pilot results and policy priorities
  • Governance: Clear roles for Ministry, regulator, and operators; data ownership and use agreed upfront
Keep the pilot concrete but adaptable. Invite input from the Sub-Committee and transport officials on corridor and metric choice.
